<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Misc._Comments">Misc. Comments</span></h2>
<p>The Mystery (or Rosary) Sonatas constitute one of the virtuoso high points of the Baroque violin literature, as in addition to the daring technical and contrapuntal demands of Biber’s writing, each sonata employs a different tuning of the violin’s strings, known as <i>scordatura</i> (the first sonata and the final passacaglia alone employ the standard violin tuning). In addition the eleventh sonata requires the violinist to cross over the middle strings at the bridge and nut to allow octave tunings between the adjacent pairs of outer strings. Each of the violin sonatas is accompanied by a figured continuo bass, whereas the passacaglia is for violin alone. The collection of sonatas survives in a manuscript copy with engravings accompanying each of the sections and portraying the depicted scene.
</p><p>A lengthy but illuminating discussion of the background for this style of composition is found at Dr James Clements’ website, entitled <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bluntinstrument.org.uk/biber/penetrating.htm">Penetrating the Mysteries</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/File:Biber_Mystery_Sonatas_scordatura.jpg" class="image" title="Tunings for the 15 Mystery Sonatas and Passacaglia"><img alt="Tunings for the 15 Mystery Sonatas and Passacaglia" src="/images/thumb/8/8d/Biber_Mystery_Sonatas_scordatura.jpg/800px-Biber_Mystery_Sonatas_scordatura.jpg" width="800" height="66" /></a>
</p>
