<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Misc._Comments">Misc. Comments</span></h2>
<ul><li>Grove Music doesn't list a double bass concerto in E major, only 2 in E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode; font-size:110%">&#x266d;</span> major, though it references others (instruments not given) that are doubtful or spurious.
</li><li>A score is published by Luck's Music Library, in which the orchestral instrumentation is 2 flutes, 2 horns, strings, cembalo.
</li><li>The Schott edition parts appear to be out of print. 
</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Well, the bass part looks like D major to me. Is it meant to be played scordatura? Or is this a D major work and is "E major" just a misreading after all? - Schissel
</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Take a look at <span class="plainlinks"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://opac.rism.info/metaopac/search?q=rism240001394&amp;db=251&amp;View=rism&amp;Language=en">RISM 240001394</a></span> - it seems there are several versions of this, the earliest copy in E<span class="music-symbol" style="font-family: Arial Unicode MS, Lucida Sans Unicode; font-size:110%">&#x266d;</span> major. I believe there was some old convention of tuning the double basses differently (scordatura) for certain keys. - Fynnjamin
</dd></dl>
</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd> This is all correct- the "Viennese tuning" used by the 5 string solo contrabass (D/F-A-d-f#-a) was often tuned up a half step to sound in Eb. The solo part was notated in D and sounded in Eb. The orchestral parts were notated and sounded in Eb. The E major editions are solely an adaptation to enable the use of modern "solo tuning" (F#-B-e-a) on a 4 string instrument tuned in 4ths. - Rechel
</dd></dl>
</dd></dl>
</dd></dl>
