<h2> <span class="mw-headline" id="Misc._Comments">Misc. Comments</span></h2>
<p>Fine's next composition, <i>Sonata for Violin and Piano</i>, is a further exploration of the energy and drive that are heard in the <i>Concertante</i> and <i>Sinfonia and Fugato</i>. This three-movement work is the first time she had used the term "sonata," and it is more in the spirit of contrast and reuse than the development and process of the traditional form. Fines <i>Sonata</i> does not exhibit a hierarchy: the violin and piano are equal partners. The <i>Sonata</i>s tonal freedom, rhythmic flexibility, and jagged contour are a return to Fines innate talent expressed in the early works, such as <i>Solo for Oboe</i>. What has changed is her ability to work on a larger scale; the <i>Sonata</i> is fifteen minutes long.
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd>Heidi Von Gunden, <i>The Music of Vivian Fine</i>, Scarecrow Press, 1999
</dd></dl>
</dd></dl>
